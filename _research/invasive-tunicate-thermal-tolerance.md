My doctoral research used the golden star tunicate, *Botryllus schlosseri*, to investigate how invasive populations respond to temperature across space and through development. This colonial marine invertebrate has spread across a broad latitudinal range, making it a useful system for separating the effects of geography, environmental history, and plasticity.

I combined common-environment experiments, physiological measurements, and population comparisons to examine variation in thermal tolerance. This work showed that both population of origin and developmental environment can shape responses to temperature, with implications for persistence and continued range expansion under changing climates.
